Itinerary

Seceda and the Odle · photo: Wolfgang Moroder / CC BY-SA 3.0

The recommended plan is "mountains to sea": Dolomites hiking while the Alps are at their best, the lead course in Arco in week 2, first leads on our own gear, home at three weeks with the goal banked. A fourth week is a stretch with a researched menu of six directions, most of them not climbing at all. A fifth week exists only if it lands on something special.

Why this order and not the reverse

Early September is the Dolomites' best fortnight and the huts and lifts start closing from about the 20th, so hiking goes first. Arco is prime all month, so the course sits comfortably in the middle, after our legs are fit and our heads are used to exposure from the via ferrata. And everything south and seaward (Kalymnos, Sicily, Montenegro's coast) only gets better as October approaches, so whatever the stretch week is, it belongs at the tail. The same dates reversed would have us on a hot island in week 1 and in a closing-down Cortina in week 5.

Weeks 1-2: Dolomites

Two bases, because there is no fast public transport between the western and eastern Dolomites. Val Gardena first (best car-free valley in the range: the guest pass makes every bus free), then across to the Cortina side for the eastern icons.

Weeks 2-3: Arco

Fri 11 - Sun 13 Sept
Settle in. A lake day at Riva or Torbole, a warm-up top-rope session, easy walks in the olive terraces. If the course is the 4-half-day format, it can start here.
Mon 14 - Wed 16 Sept
The lead course, 3 days with a UIAGM guide: clipping, lead belays, practice falls, threading anchors, bailing. The whole trip funnels through these three days.
Thu 17 Sept
Buy the kits in town with course-fresh knowledge: a rope and a rack of draws each, helmets, belay devices, and maybe fresh shoes (harnesses we already own). See Gear for the list and budget. Afternoon: first fully independent leads at Massone on our own rope.
Fri 18 - Sat 19 Sept
Consolidation days at Nago and Baone. Easy grades, perfect technique, lots of volume. Celebration dinner in the old town.
Sun 20 Sept
3-week version: train to Venice or Milan, fly home with the kit. Stretch version: set off for whichever week-4 option won the argument below.

Week 4, the stretch: pick one

By day 19 the goal is banked: course done, kit owned, first routes led. If we take a fourth week, it is pure bonus and it does not have to be climbing. Six researched directions, all reachable from Arco, all in season for late September; prices are per day for the two of us, everything included except the long-haul home.

Sicily + Aeolians, the biggest change of scene

Seven days: Catania two nights (La Pescheria fish market, granita con brioche at Savia), a guided Etna summit day (independent hikers are capped around 2,500 m; authorized guides take you to the crater zone at ~3,340 m for about €120-130), hydrofoil to Salina for two nights (Fossa delle Felci hike, Pollara sunset, Malvasia tasting), one night on Stromboli for the sunset hike to the 400 m terrace watching live explosions in the dark (the summit has been closed to everyone since 2019; ~€30-40 with Magmatrek), buffer night in Catania, fly. The hydrofoil timetable thins around 1 October, which is exactly when a 4-week version would be leaving anyway.

Montenegro, the one Sam suggested, and it holds up

Seven days: three nights in Dobrota on the Bay of Kotor (walk the Ladder of Kotor at dawn, Perast and Our Lady of the Rocks, the serpentine road up to Njeguši for prosciutto and on to the Njegoš mausoleum at 1,657 m), then drive the Piva canyon to Žabljak for three nights in Durmitor (Black Lake, Bobotov Kuk from the Sedlo pass on the best-forecast day, half-day Tara canyon rafting at ~€60-70 each). Roughly half Italian prices; a small hire car is near-essential (~€25-35/day). Two honest catches: flights in are low-frequency (the Bergamo-Podgorica route people remember no longer exists; it is Wizz from Malpensa or Ryanair to Dubrovnik plus a bus), and Kotor still gets cruise ships in late September, so town mornings are for hiking and evenings are for the old town.

Slovenia, the lowest-friction version

Six days, no airports: bus to Ljubljana, Bled for the Ojstrica viewpoint and Vintgar gorge, across the Vršič pass to Bovec on the free shuttle (it stops running 30 September, a real deadline), a canyoning day in the Soča valley (operators verified running to October, €60-70), back via the Tolmin gorges, Škocjan caves (the UNESCO underground canyon; skip Postojna, which is the mass-tourism machine), and a Ljubljana Friday night: Odprta kuhna food market, then Metelkova. Bus to Venice, fly home.

Kalymnos, if the leading has us hooked

The original extension, unchanged: fly Bergamo → Kos, ferry across, a studio each in Massouri (at €25-60 a night, separate is cheap), scooters, and the routine: climb 8:00-13:00 on morning-shade sectors (Arhi, Kasteli, Arginonta valley), swim, taverna, Fatolitis. One boat-taxi evening on Telendos, one Vathy fjord kayak day. Optional supervised day with a local instructor to check our systems. Fly home via Athens.

Dolomites round two, with an optional loud finish

Late September is the range's quietest prime: larches turning gold, stable spells, Val Gardena lifts running to November while the crowds are gone. Then, if the mood strikes, the direct Railjet from Bolzano to Munich for a one- or two-night midweek Oktoberfest hit: 2026 runs 19 September - 4 October and is an Oide Wiesn year; weekday tents keep 25% of seats unreserved, so Augustiner by 11:00 on a Monday-Wednesday works without bookings; a Maß is €14.90-15.90. It is unapologetically mass tourism, so it goes in as a controlled two-night dose, not a destination. Fly home from Munich.

Istria and Plitvice, the food pilgrimage

White truffle season peaks from late September in the Motovun forest: truffle hunting with dogs, then eating the find, at half Alba prices. Rovinj for sea swims and old-town evenings, hilltop Motovun and Grožnjan with a glass of Teran, and Plitvice's lakes at 07:30 when they are near-empty (€23 in October). Out via Zagreb; the Qatar one-stop home runs about four days a week, so the exit date needs checking before this option is chosen.

Week 5, only if it is special

The only argument for a fifth week is the twin-festival weekend of 2-4 October: the Kalymnos International Climbing Festival (we participate: clinics, excursions, films, 3,500 climbers) or Rock Master's 40th edition in Arco (we watch the world's best, free entry, gear village). Both need rooms booked well ahead, and they are mutually exclusive. If neither pulls hard, we are home at three or four weeks with the budget intact for the next trip. One anti-recommendation: Oktoberfest's final weekend also lands on 2-4 October with German Unity Day on the Saturday, its single most crushed stretch; if Oktoberfest happens at all it belongs midweek in the Dolomites chain, never in week 5.

Alternative shapes we considered

Plan B: Iberia

Picos de Europa or Ordesa hiking in weeks 1-2, then the verified 5-day "From the Gym to the Rock" course at Siurana (EUR 450 including crag transport), then Margalef or Chulilla as conditions cool. The same correct season shape as Italy. Costs a touch less; needs a car the whole way and a 4-7 hour internal drive; no single town matches Arco's course-plus-shops convenience. Lives on as the fallback if Italy pricing or availability goes wrong.

Plan C: one-country Italy

For maximum adventure-to-transit ratio: Dolomites, Arco course, then finish at Finale Ligure on the Ligurian coast for sea-cliff sport climbing and seafood, all by train and bus, no flights after landing. The 4-week version without leaving Italy. Weaker climbing-social scene than Kalymnos and some polished classic routes, but zero transit friction and superb food throughout. The 5-week version of this plan gets its own festival finale: loop back to Arco for Rock Master, 2-4 October, the sport's most storied competition in its 40th-anniversary edition, then fly home from Venice or Milan.

Plan D: course on Kalymnos instead

Dolomites weeks 1-2, fly to Kos around 12 September, a mid-September "Take the lead" session with Simon Montmory (2026 dates by enquiry), then climb on our own gear as the island cools, with the festival as the finale if we run 5 weeks. One less course-booking unknown since the dates are published, and the course itself is outstanding. The cost: hotter course days (mid-September shade tactics), gear bought on the island (fine, roughly 7 shops, but less choice than Arco), and committing to Greece before we have met the rock. This is the main live alternative to the recommendation; it is on the Decisions page.

Dates that shape the plan

  • Sat 12 Sept: Sellaronda Bike Day closes the Sella passes to cars 9:00-16:00. Fine car-free, a trap if driving that day.
  • ~20 Sept: Dolomites huts and several lifts begin closing; the hiking leg should be done by then.
  • 2-4 Oct: festival weekend, twice over. The Kalymnos climbing festival and Arco's Rock Master (40th edition) run on the same dates. A 5-week trip can end at one or the other, not both. Book rooms early either way.
