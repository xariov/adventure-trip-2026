Logistics and budget

Lago di Misurina · photo: kallerna / CC BY-SA 4.0

The five weeks run about AUD 15,700-22,600 for the two of us all-in. Most of the movement inside that range is the course format, the flight quotes and how often the splurges win. Currency notes use 1 EUR ≈ 1.64 AUD (July 2026).

Long-haul flights

The shape to book: one open-jaw ticket, Brisbane to Venice outbound and Athens to Brisbane home, on Qatar or Emirates. It prices at roughly AUD 2,000-2,400 each with the 25-30 kg baggage tier included at the base fare (Emirates gives 30 kg even on Saver for Australian routes; Qatar Classic carries 25 kg, Convenience 30 kg), the bags check through, and there is no positioning day. The alternatives lose: a plain Venice return plus a hop back from Athens (about AUD 200-300 for the Aegean or Wizz flight, plus an airport night) runs AUD 100-300 more per person and burns a full travel day, and separate full-service one-ways cost AUD 300-500 more. Budget mixed-carrier one-ways out of Athens exist from about AUD 873, but they are 30-50 hour, 3-stop, self-transfer itineraries, which is no way to move two ropes and 50 kg of luggage.

Days matter. Tuesday and Wednesday are the cheap days in both directions on these routes, so the booking is: out Tuesday 1 September (Emirates EK435 departs Brisbane 21:00, connects in Dubai, lands Venice 13:25 on the 2nd; Qatar QR899 departs 22:10 and lands Venice 14:20 via Doha, but Qatar's Doha-Venice leg currently runs only about four days a week, so confirm the Wednesday operates before choosing Qatar), home Tuesday 6 or Wednesday 7 October from Athens. Avoid the 3-4 October weekend for the flight home; it prices worse and clashes with the festival anyway. One trap: Virgin Australia's own Brisbane-Doha flights stay suspended until December, so make sure any Qatar itinerary is on QR flight numbers for the Brisbane leg.

The value alternative, if we accept its limits: China Eastern via Shanghai. Around AUD 900-1,100 one-way to Venice (a dated 20-26 September return anchored at 1,845), a civilised evening layover of about six hours, arrival in Venice at 07:40, and Australians currently transit China visa-free. The limits: the Europe fares carry only a 23 kg bag, and China Eastern cannot build the Athens open-jaw, so it only fits the inferior plain-return shape. Worth it only if the open-jaw quotes come back well above the estimates.

Timing: we are inside the optimal booking window now and September is this route's second-priciest month, so this books this week, not next month. Qatar currently has a "European summer" sale covering September travel worth checking first.

A note on rooms, since we are two friends rather than a couple: the daily figures assume sharing a twin, which prices the same as a double everywhere in Europe ("double" in the tables above is the room class, not the bed count). Separate rooms are cheap where it matters most: two studios on Kalymnos run EUR 50-90 a night total, and Montenegrin guesthouses are inexpensive enough to just take two. In the Dolomites and Arco separate rooms add roughly 30-60% to the room line, so a two-bedroom apartment is usually the better way to get our own space there. In the rifugios everyone is in dorm bunks anyway.

Insurance: answered, via ferrata included

The two questions were what climbing cover costs, and whether via ferrata needs something beyond a policy that covers sport climbing. It does. On current published wording, no mainstream Australian policy covers unguided via ferrata at Dolomites altitude: Cover-More and Freely both name via ferrata but cap it at 2,000 m, which fails Sass Rigais at 3,025 m; World Nomads names it uncapped but requires a professional guide for both via ferrata and sport climbing; Fast Cover and SCTI require licensed operators; InsureandGo and Tick name via ferrata specifically to exclude it.

The exception is insure4less, and it was live-quoted at our ages (35 and 42) on 22 July for the exact dates and all four countries. Via ferrata is automatically included on every tier, uncapped, with no guide condition, and the Rock Climbing Plan add-on covers unguided bolted sport climbing to 3,500 m. Base policy for both of us, 37 days: $298-436 depending on tier; the climbing add-on is $20.83 per person per day for only the days we nominate, so a ten-day climbing window adds about $417. Call it $730-855 total for the pair, everything on one policy. The fine print that matters: helmets mandatory at the crag, a $500 excess on climbing medical claims, no physiotherapy cover, search-and-rescue only on the top (Excel Plus) tier, and a mixed claims reputation. It is single-trip and must be bought before leaving Australia. Also worth knowing: their age bands step at 36, not 40, so crossing 40 changes nothing (the 42-year-old pays about $41 more than the 35-year-old on the mid tier).

The belt-and-braces addition, worthwhile whichever base policy wins: Austrian Alpine Club membership through the UK Britannia section, GBP 65 plus GBP 50 for a partner rate (about AUD 220-250 for the pair, joinable by Australians, covering immediately). It covers via ferrata and unguided climbing outright, with EUR 25,000 mountain rescue per person and unlimited repatriation in the first eight weeks of a trip, which are exactly the catastrophic items retail policies condition away, plus discounts at the Alta Via rifugios. Its cap is EUR 10,000 of foreign hospital cover, and Australians cannot buy the premium top-up; in Italy the Australia-Italy reciprocal health agreement (Medicare card plus passport, public hospitals) largely plugs that, and Italy is where the via ferrata days are.

The rest of the trip's activities fall out cleanly under insure4less: trekking is automatically covered to 4,000 m without a guide, whitewater rafting to grade 4, and canyoning when run by a commercial operator, which the Tara and Soča trips are.

Booking order

This week
Book the open-jaw flights (out Tue 1 Sept, home Tue 6 or Wed 7 Oct); quote Qatar and Emirates the same day and check Qatar's Doha-Venice leg operates on the Wednesday. Buy insurance the same day so cancellation cover starts. Email the Arco guides with dates and the syllabus checklist. If the hut traverse appeals, those emails go out now too; the contact table is on Itinerary.
By early August
Book refundable Kalymnos rooms covering 30 Sept - 5 Oct (festival week fills first), Val Gardena and Cortina-side rooms, and the Arco apartment. Decide the Dolomites car question before hire prices climb.
Mid August - early September
Tirana-Athens and Athens-Kos flights once the corridor firms up (three to six weeks out is the price sweet spot; availability holds). Seceda lift slots and the Tre Cime toll slot. Any guided via ferrata day with Catores.
On the road
Kotor car hire and rooms a few days out; the Kotor-Shkodra bus the day before; the Koman ferry chain online one or two days ahead; Albanian guesthouses by WhatsApp with euro cash in hand.
