Decisions

Everything still open, with the options, a recommendation, and the date it stops being free to defer. Work down the list in order; the early ones unlock the later ones. This page gets updated as calls are made.

1. How long is the trip?

Open Decide by: end of July, before booking flights

  • a) 3 weeks (home ~20 Sept): the full arc. Dolomites at their best, the course done, the kit bought, first independent leads climbed. The goal (leave with skills and gear) is met in full; Australia has crags for the rest.
  • b) 4 weeks (home ~27-28 Sept): the stretch. One extra week, and it does not have to be climbing: Kalymnos as the island comes good is the climbing answer; Slovenia, Montenegro or Sicily are the exploration answers. Roughly AUD 1,600-2,300 extra all-in for the two of us. The menu is on Itinerary.
  • c) 5 weeks (home ~5 Oct): only if it aligns with something special. 2026 obliges: the Kalymnos climbing festival and Arco's Rock Master 40th are both on 2-4 October. No special alignment, no week 5.

Recommendation: plan a as the base and keep b live until the flights are booked. The trip is complete at 3 weeks. The stretch week is worth it only if one of the tail options genuinely excites us; decide that before booking, because changing a return date later usually costs more than deciding now. Take c only by deliberately aiming at the 2-4 October weekend.

2. Where do we do the course?

Open Decide by: end of July, courses and studios book out

  • a) Arco, week 2-3: prime season, cheapest UIAGM instruction found anywhere (EUR 170-340 for stacked courses, ~EUR 700-855 for a private 3-day), dates on request, and the best gear-buying street in Europe on the doorstep. Course first, island later with skills already consolidated.
  • b) Kalymnos, mid-September: Simon Montmory's "Take the lead", the most complete packaged syllabus found, EUR 745 each, 2026 dates by enquiry. Hotter course days and it commits us to Greece before we have climbed outdoors.

Recommendation: a. Arco wins on season, price, flexibility and the gear-shop moment. Option b remains genuinely good; if the trip stays at 3 weeks and we want a packaged everything-sorted course, it deserves a second look as Plan D on the Itinerary page.

3. Dolomites: valley bases or hut-to-hut?

Open Decide by: this month, rifugios are booking now

  • a) Two valley bases + day hikes: Val Gardena then Cortina, every big-ticket hike from the list, total flexibility when a weather front rolls through, real beds and showers every night.
  • b) Add a 4-day Alta Via 1 section: Passo Falzarego to Passo Duran, the most scenic stretch and the easiest to book. Half board €55-80 pp/night, separate email to every hut, deposits often non-refundable, cash needed.

Recommendation: a, with b as an upgrade if the hut emails come back easy. The day-hike version loses almost none of the scenery and keeps weather flexibility in the one leg where weather matters most. If the hut evenings appeal (they are special), emails go out this week or not at all.

4. Car or car-free in Italy?

Open Decide by: mid-August, hire prices climb

  • a) Car-free: Val Gardena guest pass makes all valley and South Tyrol buses free, Cortina Express runs from Venice, bus 444 serves Tre Cime, and Arco needs no car at all. Cheaper, no pass-parking fees, no Sellaronda Bike Day trap on 12 Sept.
  • b) Hire car throughout: ~US$25-45/day plus the €40 Tre Cime toll slot and parking. Buys Val di Funes convenience and rainy-day range.
  • c) Hybrid: car-free valleys, one-day rental or taxi for the awkward bits.

Recommendation: a. This specific route is the one part of the Alps genuinely built for car-free hiking, and the transfer days are simpler without a drop-off fee between Italy and a Greek flight.

5. If we stretch: where does week 4 go?

Open Decide with question 1, before flights: the choice changes the exit airport

The goal (skills and gear) is complete by week 3, so the stretch week does not have to be climbing. The full researched menu with day sketches is on Itinerary; the short version:

  • a) Sicily + Aeolians: guided Etna summit at ~3,340 m, Stromboli's explosions from the 400 m terrace at dusk, Salina, the best food of the trip, sea at 24-25°. €200-280/day for two.
  • b) Montenegro: Bay of Kotor + Durmitor + Tara rafting, roughly half Italian prices (€110-160/day including the car). The catch is low-frequency flights in and the least convenient journey home.
  • c) Slovenia: zero flights, Soča canyoning verified running, Škocjan caves, easy Emirates exit via Venice. €180-260/day.
  • d) Kalymnos: consolidate the new skill on the island built for new leaders, cheapest of all (€90-140/day). The right call only if we finish the course hungry for more rock.

Two wildcards also researched and on the itinerary page: a quiet golden Dolomites week chained into a midweek Oktoberfest hit (fly home from Munich), and Istria's white-truffle season with Plitvice (out via Zagreb).

Recommendation: a if we want the biggest change of scene, b if adventure-per-dollar wins. Both are exploration-first, which matches the brief. d stays right if the leading is addictive by then, and c is the answer if we cannot face another airport. No wrong doors here; this is the fun argument to have.

6. Festival finish or not?

Open Only matters for the 5-week version

The weekend of 2-4 October 2026 has two climbing festivals at once, so this choice follows from question 5: whichever place the tail is in, that is the festival on offer.

  • a) Kalymnos International Climbing Festival, 2-4 Oct: clinics, films, excursions, 3,500 climbers, the social peak of the island's season, and we are participants, not spectators. Book the studio now; the island fills.
  • b) Rock Master, Arco, 2-4 Oct: the sport's most storied competition in its 40th-anniversary edition, free entry, gear-village expo, pro meet-and-greets. Only fits the Italy-only Plan C shape. World-class, but we mostly watch.
  • c) Neither: quiet crags to the end, home by 2 October.

Recommendation: the festival IS the only reason week 5 exists. If we take a fifth week at all, it is because we deliberately aimed at this weekend: a if the stretch went to Kalymnos, b if it stayed in Italy. Otherwise home at three or four weeks, and c is not a loss.

7. Gear: where do we buy?

Open Settled by course choice; no purchase before the course

  • a) Full kit in Arco, day after the course: 17 shops in 300 m, course-fresh knowledge, guide's advice on models, European prices.
  • b) Buy at home before flying: pay Australian prices and haul 15+ kg both ways for no benefit.
  • c) Split: shoes and harnesses fitted at home if we find good deals, rope and hardware in Arco.

Recommendation: a (or c if a shoe sale appears before we fly). Details and the itemised budget are on Gear.
