Destinations

Lago di Sorapis · photo: kallerna / CC BY-SA 4.0

Our window starts at the tail of European summer, and that decides more than anything else does. Alpine hiking is best in the first half of September and the huts and lifts start closing from about the 20th, while the Mediterranean crags only become pleasant as the heat fades in the second half. Any plan that works has mountains early and the warm places late. Italy with a southern tail does this naturally; so would Slovenia or Spain. Turkey has it backwards, with nothing good before October, and Canada compresses it, with everything needing to finish by the start of October.

The core picks

Dolomites, Italy

Hiking coreweeks 1-2

The best hiking in Europe for our money, in its best fortnight. Tre Cime, Seceda, Lago di Sorapis, Croda da Lago, the Lagazuoi war tunnels, Alpe di Siusi, and via ferrata as a bridge toward the climbing. The rifugio food is a reason to go in itself.

Base in Val Gardena, which works fully without a car (guest-pass buses are free), plus a few nights near Cortina for the eastern peaks. Twin room €130-220.

Arco / Lake Garda, Italy

Course homeweek 2-3

Where European sport climbing more or less began, 2-3 hours from the Dolomites, and September is its best season. UIAGM-guided lead courses from EUR 170, seventeen or so gear shops along 300 m of old town, and Massone, Baone and Nago close by for first leads. The lake is there for the rest days.

Course, shops and easy crags in one small town. See Lead course.

Kalymnos, Greece

Stretch optionweek 4-5, if climbing wins

About half of the island's 3,400+ routes are 6b+ or easier, the bolting is close and modern, studios cost €30-45 a night, and the evenings are climbers comparing notes at Fatolitis bar. The 2026 international climbing festival runs 2-4 October.

Best from late September, which is exactly when a fourth or fifth week would land. €90-120 a day for the two of us, all-in.

Slovenia

Swing option

Julian Alps hiking, the Soča valley for canyoning and rafting, and Osp and Mišja Peč, whose climbing season opens in September (the big cave at Osp stays 27-28° on hot days). Osp is 30 minutes from Trieste, so it folds into an Italy loop without flying. Runs 10-30% cheaper than Italy.

A stretch-week contender: no flights from Arco, adventure sports running to October, easy exit via Venice.

The stretch-week tails

If we take a fourth week it doesn't have to be climbing. Slovenia and Kalymnos above both compete for it, and so do these four, researched to the same standard. Day sketches, transit and exit airports for all six are on Itinerary.

Sicily + Aeolians, Italy

Volcanoes

Two live volcanoes in one week: a guided Etna summit at around 3,340 m, and Stromboli's explosions watched from the 400 m terrace at dusk. Salina sits in between, the eating is the best of any option on this site, and the sea is still 24-25°.

€200-280 a day for two. A cheap 1.5 h flight in; flydubai home from Catania Wed/Fri/Sun.

Montenegro

Cheapest adventure

The Bay of Kotor first (the Ladder of Kotor at dawn, Perast, the Lovćen mausoleum at 1,657 m), then Durmitor for Bobotov Kuk and a raft down the Tara canyon. Proper mountains and a warm sea, with few people around once the cruise ships leave for the day.

€110-160 a day for two including the hire car, roughly half Italian prices. The catch is low-frequency flights in and out.

Istria + Plitvice, Croatia

Truffle season

White truffle season peaks from late September around Motovun: go out with the dogs, then eat what they found, at half of what the same plate costs in Alba. Rovinj for sea swims, hilltop wine villages, and Plitvice's lakes at 07:30 before the buses arrive.

€180-260 a day for two. Ferry or bus from Venice; out via Zagreb (Qatar, ~4x weekly).

Dolomites II + Munich

Mountains, then Munich

The Dolomites in their quietest week of the year, larches turning and lifts still running, then the direct train to Munich for a midweek day or two at Oktoberfest before flying home from there.

€220-300 a day for two in the mountains; the Munich nights are the expensive part. No flights until the one home.

Why the losers lost

Squamish deserved a closer look than most. Early September there is about as good as granite gets, and courses exist (Altus and Squamish Rock Guides run 1-day modules at CAD 190-200). But it's the most expensive option on the table, late September brings a real chance of settled rain, the local course format is stacked single days rather than a proper multi-day lead course, and the scene leans toward trad granite when what we want is a sport kit for European limestone. Right trip, wrong year.

South Africa surprised us. Waterval Boven is the best sport crag in Africa, with a resident school whose intro course covers leading, September is southern spring, the Drakensberg is nearby, and it would have been the cheapest option researched, with Kruger two hours up the road. It lost on the eating and the general density of things to see, on the driving and the security awareness needed around Gauteng, and on thin gear shopping. If this trip were purely about adventure per dollar it would have won.

Turkey's season starts in October, and the Lycian Way pushes past 30° all September, so it's backwards for our dates. It keeps a distant claim on an early-October tail via Antalya, but the stretch-week options now beat it.

France works in September, but the English-language courses skew toward alpine multi-pitch out of Chamonix at EUR 395-990, the Verdon is a committing abseil-in environment that's the wrong place to learn to lead, and it costs more than Italy for the same quality.

Spain came closest. Picos de Europa or Ordesa for the hiking, then the 5-day "From the Gym to the Rock" course at Siurana for EUR 450 with crag transport included. It lost narrowly: the Catalan crags are still hot in early September, the drive between the hiking and the climbing is four to seven hours, a car is needed throughout, and no single town matches Arco's course-plus-shops setup. It stays on the sheet as Plan B if Italy pricing goes wrong.

Meteora gets a special mention because people will suggest it. The hiking and the monastery scenery are worth a stop, and there is climbing, but it's runout conglomerate with bolts 10 m apart, described by UKC as a sport area for trad climbers. Not somewhere for new leaders to climb independently; a guided morning is the way to touch the rock if we pass through.

Via ferrata shortlist

  • Lagazuoi tunnels / Kaiserjäger (grade A/B): self-guided with rented kits, €15-35 a day from Snow Service Cortina or the Falzarego rental.
  • Col dei Bos (Falzarego): the standard first proper ferrata; a guided day runs about €175 with Alpinschule Dreizinnen.
  • Piz da Lech (Corvara, grade C): gondola-assisted 3-4 h round trip with two vertical ladder pitches; the lift runs to 4 October.
  • Sass Rigais (3,025 m, Val Gardena): a summit ferrata that suits people without much prior experience; Catores guides it at €170 pp (min 3, surcharge for 2).
  • Tridentina (Passo Gardena): sustained, popular, suspension-bridge finish at Rifugio Pisciadù; Catores €155 pp.

Hut-to-hut

A 3-4 day Alta Via 1 section in early September is still bookable as of mid-July, but only just. There's no central platform; each rifugio books separately, and the flagship northern huts (Lagazuoi, Sennes) fill months out. The central section from Passo Falzarego to Passo Duran is the most scenic stretch and the easiest to book. Half board runs €55-80 per person a night, deposits are often non-refundable, and many huts are cash-only. The fallback loses very little: valley bases and the day-hike list above. If we want the hut version, the emails go out this month or not at all. See Decisions.

Kalymnos detail

The island suits new leaders better than anywhere else we looked at. Arhi and the Arginonta valley have more routes at 5c-6a than any other sector on the island, Kasteli is the standard first-trip crag, the Afternoon slabs and the easy lines at Odyssey sit next to the famous caves, and Glaros on Telendos stays shaded all day and is reached by boat taxi. The heat in mid-September is workable rather than pleasant: climb 8:00 to 13:00 on morning-shade sectors, swim through the middle of the day (the sea is 24-25°), then finish on a late-shade wall. By early October it's simply good.

Two things to know before choosing it. The 2026 festival runs 2-4 October, with clinics and several thousand climbers; staying for it is a decision in itself, since the island books out around it. And after a bolt failure caused a fatal accident in March 2026, the island accelerated rebolting with titanium glue-ins; we'd stick to popular, recently equipped routes, which as new leaders we would anyway. If we want supervision for the first days on our own gear, Simon Montmory takes private sessions (half-day around €270) and Climb in Kalymnos runs courses aimed at gym climbers going outside.
