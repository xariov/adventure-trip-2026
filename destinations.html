Destinations

Every serious candidate for the window from 29 August, compared against what we actually want: the lead course, big hiking, warm-but-not-hot weather, great food, and value. The honest alternatives are here too, including the ones we ruled out and why.

The season physics decide the shape

Our window starts at the tail of European summer. Alpine hiking (Dolomites, Julian Alps, Pyrenees) is at its best in the first half: early September is prime, and huts and lifts start closing from about 20 September. Mediterranean climbing (Kalymnos, Slovenian Istria, Catalonia) opens up in the second half as the heat fades. So the right shape for this trip is mountains first, then follow the good weather south. Italy plus an optional Greek tail does this perfectly; so would Slovenia or Spain. Turkey has it backwards (nothing is good until October) and Canada has it compressed (everything must finish by the start of October).

The core picks

Dolomites, Italy

Hiking coreweeks 1-2

The best hiking scenery in Europe, hit in its best fortnight. Tre Cime, Seceda, Lago di Sorapis, Croda da Lago, the Lagazuoi war tunnels, Alpe di Siusi, and via ferrata as a bridge toward climbing. Rifugio food is a genuine highlight, not a compromise.

Base: Val Gardena (car-free heaven, free guest-pass buses) plus a few nights near Cortina for the eastern icons. Double room €130-220.

Arco / Lake Garda, Italy

Course homeweek 2-3

The birthplace of European sport climbing, 2-3 hours from the Dolomites. September is prime season. UIAGM-guided lead courses from EUR 170, roughly 17 gear shops along 300 m of old town, and Massone, Baone and Nago for first leads. Swim in the lake between crag days.

Everything the trip's anchor needs in one small town. See Lead course.

Kalymnos, Greece

Extension pickweeks 4-5

Purpose-built for new leaders: about half of the 3,400+ routes are 6b+ or easier, bolting is close and modern, studios cost €30-45 a night, and the evening scene is climbers swapping beta at Fatolitis bar. The 2026 international climbing festival runs 2-4 October.

Best from late September, which is exactly when a 4th or 5th week would land. €90-120 a day for the two of us, all-in.

Slovenia

Swing option

Julian Alps hiking, the Soča valley for canyoning and rafting, and Osp/Mišja Peč, whose prime climbing season opens in September (the big cave stays 27-28° even on hot days). Osp is 30 minutes from Trieste, so it folds into the Italy loop with zero flights. Roughly 10-30% cheaper than Italy.

Could replace the Greek tail, or claim 4-5 days between Arco and a flight out of Venice.

Why the losers lost

  • Squamish: early September is genuinely world-class there, and courses exist (Altus, Squamish Rock Guides, ~CAD 190-200 per day-module). But it is the most expensive option on the table, late September is a real rain gamble, the local course format is stacked 1-day modules rather than an integrated lead course, and the scene skews trad granite when our goal is a sport kit for Europe-style limestone. Wonderful trip, wrong year.
  • South Africa: the honest surprise of the research. Waterval Boven is Africa's best sport crag with a resident school (Roc 'n Rope) whose intro course explicitly covers leading, September is prime southern spring, Drakensberg hiking is superb, and it would be the cheapest option researched, with Kruger two hours away. It lost on the food-and-sights density we want, mandatory driving with security awareness around Gauteng, and a thinner gear-buying experience. If the trip were about maximum adventure per dollar, this would win.
  • Turkey: Geyikbayırı's season starts in October and the Lycian Way pushes 30°+ in September. Backwards for our window. Keeps a claim on the last week of a 5-week version (fly Antalya early October), competing with Kalymnos for that slot.
  • France: September works and the Verdon is iconic, but the English-language course scene skews alpine multi-pitch (Chamonix, EUR 395-990), the Verdon is a committing abseil-in environment that is wrong for first leads, and costs run above Italy for the same quality.
  • Spain: the strongest true alternative. Picos de Europa or Ordesa hiking in early September, then the verified "From the Gym to the Rock" 5-day course at Siurana (EUR 450 including crag transport). It lost narrowly: early September at the Catalan crags is still hot, the hiking-to-climbing drive is 4-7 hours, a car is mandatory throughout, and no single town matches Arco's course-plus-shops convenience. It stays on the sheet as Plan B if Italy pricing goes wrong.
  • Meteora, Greece (special mention): magnificent hiking and monastery scenery, and yes there is climbing, but it is runout conglomerate with 10 m+ bolt spacing, described as "a sport climbing area for trad climbers". As new leaders we do not climb there independently; a guided taster morning is the honest option if we pass through.

Via ferrata shortlist (the bridge to climbing)

  • Lagazuoi tunnels / Kaiserjäger (grade A/B): self-guided warm-up with rented kit, ~€15-35/day from Snow Service Cortina or the Falzarego rental.
  • Col dei Bos (Falzarego): the classic first proper ferrata; guided full day about €175 with Alpinschule Dreizinnen.
  • Piz da Lech (Corvara, grade C): gondola-assisted 3-4 h round trip with two vertical ladder pitches; lift runs to 4 October.
  • Sass Rigais (3,025 m, Val Gardena): summit ferrata rated ideal without much experience; Catores guides it at €170 pp (min 3, surcharge for 2).
  • Tridentina (Passo Gardena): famous, sustained, suspension-bridge finish at Rifugio Pisciadù; Catores €155 pp.

Hut-to-hut reality check

A 3-4 day Alta Via 1 section in early September is still bookable as of mid-July, but tight: no central platform, each rifugio books separately, and the flagship northern huts (Lagazuoi, Sennes) fill months out. The central section from Passo Falzarego to Passo Duran is the most scenic stretch and the easiest to book. Half board runs €55-80 per person per night, deposits often non-refundable, many huts cash-only. Fallback that loses very little: valley bases plus the day-hike list above. If we want hut-to-hut, the emails go out this month. See Decisions.

Kalymnos detail: why the extension writes itself

  • Made for new leaders: more 5c-6a than anywhere else at Arhi and the Arginonta valley, first-trip-friendly Kasteli, the Afternoon slabs, easy lines at Odyssey next to the famous hard caves, and Glaros on Telendos (all-day shade, reached by boat taxi).
  • Heat is managed, not suffered: climb 8:00-13:00 on morning-shade sectors, swim at midday (sea at 24-25°), late lines at Afternoon or Summertime. By early October it is simply pleasant.
  • Festival decision: the 2026 Kalymnos International Climbing Festival runs 2-4 October, with clinics and 3,500+ climbers in the 2025 edition. Staying for it is a big social payoff; leaving before it keeps the island quiet. Either way, book the studio early.
  • Safety note, honestly: after a bolt-failure accident in March 2026 the island accelerated rebolting with titanium glue-ins. We stick to popular, recently-equipped routes, which as new leaders we would anyway.
  • Supervision on tap: Simon Montmory (private coaching, half-day around €270), Climb in Kalymnos (courses for indoor climbers going outside), and festival clinics if we overlap it.
