Logistics and budget

Lago di Misurina · photo: kallerna / CC BY-SA 4.0

The whole trip runs AUD 13,700-17,800 for the two of us at three weeks, with each extra week adding AUD 1,500-2,300. Most of the movement inside those ranges comes from flights and the course format. Currency notes use 1 EUR ≈ 1.64 AUD (July 2026).

Long-haul flights

Since the Middle East disruption that began in February 2026, Australia-Europe economy fares are running roughly 20-30% above normal and some Gulf-carrier frequencies are reduced. Specifically for us: Virgin Australia's Brisbane-Doha flights are suspended until 8 December 2026 and Qatar Airways' own Brisbane frequency is reduced, so verify any Qatar-coded itinerary actually operates before paying for it. Singapore Airlines and Cathay Pacific route around the region entirely.

Venice and Milan fares overlap almost completely; Milan's floor is maybe AUD 50-150 lower, but Venice is the better Dolomites gateway. Fly into Venice unless a Milan fare is far cheaper.

Open-jaw tickets (for the four- and five-week versions) usually add only 0-10% to the fare, call it AUD 100-300 each, but the exit airport depends on the stretch-week choice: Athens for Kalymnos, Catania for Sicily (flydubai to Dubai runs Wed/Fri/Sun only), Dubrovnik for Montenegro (Emirates via Dubai; verify October operating days), Venice for Slovenia, Munich for the Oktoberfest chain, Zagreb for Istria (Qatar, about 4x weekly). Carrier catch: Singapore Airlines and Cathay serve none of the small ones, so single-ticket open-jaws lean on Emirates or Qatar. The robust fallback for any tail: a cheap hop back to Milan or Venice and the plain Italy return. Decide the tail before booking the long haul.

A note on rooms, since we are two friends rather than a couple: the daily figures assume sharing a twin, which prices the same as a double everywhere in Europe ("double" in the tables above is the room class, not the bed count). Separate rooms are cheap where it matters most: two studios on Kalymnos run EUR 50-90 a night total, and Montenegrin guesthouses are inexpensive enough to just take two. In the Dolomites and Arco separate rooms add roughly 30-60% to the room line, so a two-bedroom apartment is usually the better way to get our own space there. In the rifugios everyone is in dorm bunks anyway.

Insurance that covers what we are actually doing

Most Australian policies exclude roped climbing or quietly require a licensed guide, and after the course we are exactly the people that clause bites. The wording matters more than the price.

  • Freely (Cover-More's app brand), shortlisted: adventure add-on covers rock climbing "for bolted routes and using ropes and/or guides" up to 4,000 m. The "and/or" is the phrase that covers unguided sport climbing. Roughly AUD 400-700 base for the two of us plus a few dollars per climbing day.
  • Cover-More adventure pack, shortlisted: covers unguided bolted climbing under 2,000 m; fine for Arco and Kalymnos, tight for high Dolomites via ferrata days, so check that leg specifically.
  • insure4less rock climbing plan: dedicated sport-climbing add-on to 3,500 m; quote-based.
  • World Nomads, caution: the activity list looks right, but the Australian help pages flag a professional-guide condition that would gut cover for independent climbing. Only with a current PDS check.
  • Fast Cover and SCTI: no - both require a licensed operator or instructor.
  • If the stretch week is Sicily or Montenegro: check the trekking clauses too, not just the climbing ones. The guided Etna summit stands at 3,340 m (over Cover-More's 2,000 m climbing cap, and near common trekking altitude limits) and Bobotov Kuk's final scramble uses fixed cables; confirm both are inside the chosen policy's activity list before booking.
  • Worth adding regardless: Austrian Alpine Club membership (joinable by Australians, ~EUR 135 a year for two) includes mountain rescue to EUR 25,000 each, repatriation, and discounts at Alta Via rifugios. Cheap backstop; not a replacement for travel insurance (no cancellation or baggage cover, and the 24-hour emergency line must be called first).

Booking deadlines

This week
Settle trip length and course location (Decisions 1 and 2). If hut-to-hut appeals, the Alta Via rifugio emails go out now or the option lapses.
By end of July
Book long-haul flights (fares are elevated and not falling). Book or reserve the course: email the Arco guides with dates and the syllabus checklist, or get "Take the lead" session dates from Simon Montmory and hold one with the 30% deposit. Buy insurance the same day as flights so cancellation cover starts immediately.
Early August
Book Val Gardena and Cortina-side rooms (early September is shoulder season but the good-value places go first) and the Arco apartment. If the festival finish is on: book the Kalymnos studio now; October is the island's peak month.
Mid August
If stretching: book the week-4 leg (Bergamo-Kos, Bergamo/Venice-Catania, Malpensa-Podgorica or the Munich train; all are low-frequency or price-climbing routes). Reserve the Tre Cime toll slot or plan the Dobbiaco bus. Book any guided via ferrata day.
Before flying
Austrian Alpine Club membership, offline maps, rifugio cash float, and a folder with every booking. Leave a rope-sized hole in one duffel.
