Decisions

Work down the list in order; the early calls unlock the later ones, and each comes with the date it stops being free to put off. This page gets updated as we make them.

1. How long is the trip?

Open Decide by end of July

  • a) 3 weeks (home ~20 Sept): the full arc. Dolomites at their best, the course done, the kit bought, first independent leads climbed. The goal (leave with skills and gear) is met in full; Australia has crags for the rest.
  • b) 4 weeks (home ~27-28 Sept): one extra week, climbing or not: Kalymnos if we want more rock; Sicily, Montenegro, Slovenia and others if we do not. Roughly AUD 1,600-2,300 extra all-in for the two of us. The options are on Itinerary.
  • c) 5 weeks (home ~5 Oct): only if it is aimed at something specific, and this year there is something: the Kalymnos climbing festival and Arco's Rock Master both run 2-4 October. Nothing to aim at, no week five.

Plan a as the base and keep b alive until the flights are booked. The trip is complete at three weeks, so the stretch week only earns its place if one of the options actually excites us, and that call is cheaper made before booking than changed after. c happens only by aiming at the 2-4 October weekend on purpose.

2. Where do we do the course?

Open Decide by end of July

  • a) Arco, week 2-3: prime season, cheapest UIAGM instruction found anywhere (EUR 170-340 for stacked courses, ~EUR 700-855 for a private 3-day), dates on request, and the best gear-buying street in Europe on the doorstep. Course first, island later with skills already consolidated.
  • b) Kalymnos, mid-September: Simon Montmory's "Take the lead", the most complete packaged syllabus found, EUR 745 each, 2026 dates by enquiry. Hotter course days and it commits us to Greece before we have climbed outdoors.

a is the stronger booking: Arco wins on season, price, flexibility and the shops. b is still a good course, and if the trip stays at three weeks and we would rather have one person package everything, it deserves a second look as Plan D on Itinerary.

3. Dolomites: valley bases or hut-to-hut?

Open Decide this month

  • a) Two valley bases + day hikes: Val Gardena then Cortina, every big-ticket hike from the list, total flexibility when a weather front rolls through, real beds and showers every night.
  • b) Add a 4-day Alta Via 1 section: Passo Falzarego to Passo Duran, the most scenic stretch and the easiest to book. Half board €55-80 pp/night, separate email to every hut, deposits often non-refundable, cash needed.

Take a, with b as an upgrade if the hut emails come back easy. The day-hike version loses almost none of the scenery and keeps flexibility in the one leg where weather matters most. If the hut evenings appeal, the emails go out this week or not at all.

4. Car or car-free in Italy?

Open Decide by mid-August

  • a) Car-free: Val Gardena guest pass makes all valley and South Tyrol buses free, Cortina Express runs from Venice, bus 444 serves Tre Cime, and Arco needs no car at all. Cheaper, no pass-parking fees, no Sellaronda Bike Day trap on 12 Sept.
  • b) Hire car throughout: ~US$25-45/day plus the €40 Tre Cime toll slot and parking. Buys Val di Funes convenience and rainy-day range.
  • c) Hybrid: car-free valleys, one-day rental or taxi for the awkward bits.

Car-free suits this route: this corner of the Alps is set up for it better than anywhere else we looked, and the transfer days are simpler with no car to return before whichever tail leg follows.

5. If we stretch: where does week 4 go?

Open Decide with question 1

The goal (skills and gear) is complete by week 3, so the stretch week does not have to be climbing. This call also sets the exit airport, which is why it comes before booking flights. The full researched menu with day sketches is on Itinerary; the short version:

  • a) Sicily + Aeolians: guided Etna summit at ~3,340 m, Stromboli's explosions from the 400 m terrace at dusk, Salina, the best food of the trip, sea at 24-25°. €200-280/day for two.
  • b) Montenegro: Bay of Kotor + Durmitor + Tara rafting, roughly half Italian prices (€110-160/day including the car). The catch is low-frequency flights in and the least convenient journey home.
  • c) Slovenia: zero flights, Soča canyoning verified running, Škocjan caves, easy Emirates exit via Venice. €180-260/day.
  • d) Kalymnos: consolidate the new skill on the island built for new leaders, cheapest of all (€90-140/day). The right call only if we finish the course hungry for more rock.

Two wildcards also researched and on the itinerary page: a quiet golden Dolomites week chained into a midweek Oktoberfest hit (fly home from Munich), and Istria's white-truffle season with Plitvice (out via Zagreb).

For the biggest change of scene, a; for the most adventure per dollar, b. d makes sense if we finish the course wanting more rock, and c if we cannot face another airport. There is no bad pick on this list.

6. Festival finish or not?

Open 5-week version only

The weekend of 2-4 October 2026 has two climbing festivals at once, so this choice follows from question 5: whichever place the tail is in, that is the festival on offer.

  • a) Kalymnos International Climbing Festival, 2-4 Oct: clinics, films, excursions, 3,500 climbers, the social peak of the island's season, and we are participants, not spectators. Book the studio now; the island fills.
  • b) Rock Master, Arco, 2-4 Oct: the sport's most storied competition in its 40th-anniversary edition, free entry, gear-village expo, pro meet-and-greets. Only fits the Italy-only Plan C shape. World-class, but we mostly watch.
  • c) Neither: quiet crags to the end, home by 2 October.

This weekend is the only reason a fifth week exists. If we take one, it is because we aimed at it on purpose: a if the stretch went to Kalymnos, b if it stayed in Italy. Otherwise we are home at three or four weeks and have lost nothing.

7. Gear: where and when do we buy?

Open Where and timing both open

One kit each (we both want our own gear), and harnesses and shoes are already owned, so this is about ropes, racks and hardware.

Where:

  • a) Both kits in Arco: 17 shops in 300 m, European prices, and a EUR 1,200+ two-kit basket to negotiate with.
  • b) At home before flying: pay Australian prices and haul ~15 kg both ways for no benefit.
  • c) Shoes topped up at home first: only if a genuine sale appears; otherwise Arco is La Sportiva's backyard and fits shoes properly.

When, if it is Arco:

  • a) Just before the course: learn on our own gear, guide checks the purchases, belay-device muscle memory lands on the device we own. Needs the provider's OK to run the course on our equipment; ask when booking.
  • b) Just after: try the instructor's gear first, buy with course-sharpened opinions.
  • c) Split: personal hardware before, ropes and racks whenever we feel ready.

On where, a. On when, the trade-offs are laid out on Gear and the call is ours; the current lean is before the course.
